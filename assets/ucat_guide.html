03
VR

Verbal Reasoning

44 Q
Count
22 min
Time
30 s
Per Q

Eleven passages, four questions each, and only half a minute per question. You cannot read every word — VR is a test of controlled skimming and disciplined logic, judged only on what the passage says.

What it tests

  • Comprehension and inference from dense, often dull text (science, history, policy).
  • Separating what is stated from what is merely plausible or true in the real world.

The two question types

  • True / False / Can’t Tell: is a statement supported, contradicted, or simply not determinable from the passage?
  • Free-text / inference: “the author would most agree…”, “which statement is best supported…” — pick the option grounded in the text.

Strategy that moves the needle

  • Question first, then hunt. Read the statement, lift its keywords, scan the passage for them, decide. Don’t pre-read the whole passage.
  • Budget ~2 minutes per passage. A stopwatch in your head beats a per-question one here.
  • Decode Can’t Tell: if answering needs outside knowledge or an assumption the text never makes, it’s Can’t Tell, not True.

Common traps

  • Absolute words — “all”, “never”, “only”, “always”. One counter-example in the text makes them False.
  • Your own knowledge. If it’s true in life but unstated in the passage, it isn’t True here.
  • Sinking three minutes into one brutal passage. Flag it, guess, move.

Worked micro-example

Passage: “The clinic opened a second site in 2019 to reduce waiting times.” Statement: Waiting times fell after 2019.
Answer: Can’t Tell. The passage gives the intent (“to reduce”), never the outcome. Tempting as True — but nothing states times actually fell.
⚠“Reduce” describes a goal, not a result. Reading purpose as fact is the #1 VR error.
04
DM

Decision Making

35 Q
Count
37 min
Time
63 s
Per Q

The most time-generous subtest — over a minute per question — and the one that rewards careful, structured reasoning over speed. Each question stands alone. Use the noteboard.

What it tests

  • Logical puzzles and deductions from a set of conditions.
  • Syllogisms — which conclusions must follow from given statements.
  • Venn diagrams and set interpretation.
  • Recognising assumptions and picking the strongest argument.
  • Probability and basic statistical reasoning.

The scoring quirk to exploit

  • Some questions present five statements to mark Yes/No each. Get all right for full marks, most right for partial — so grind these out; even a near-miss scores.

Strategy that moves the needle

  • Draw it. Venn diagrams and logic grids on the noteboard beat holding it in your head.
  • “Strongest argument” = directly relevant + no logical flaw + addresses the actual question. Reject anything that generalises or answers a different question, even if it “sounds” sensible.
  • Syllogisms: accept only conclusions that are necessarily true, never just probable.
  • Spend the time you’re given. This is where accuracy pays — don’t rush what’s deliberately paced.

Worked micro-example

“All members of the team are qualified. Some qualified people are first-aiders.” Conclusion: Some team members are first-aiders.
Answer: No. The team are all qualified, but the qualified first-aiders needn’t include any team member. It could be true — but it doesn’t have to be.
⚠“Could be true” ≠ “must be true”. DM only rewards necessity.
05
QR

Quantitative Reasoning

36 Q
Count
26 min
Time
43 s
Per Q

The maths is only GCSE-level — the difficulty is doing it accurately in 43 seconds while reading a chart. Fluency with percentages and a fast hand on the on-screen calculator is worth more than any clever trick.

What it tests

  • Percentages, percentage change and reverse percentages.
  • Ratios, proportion and best-value comparisons.
  • Rates — speed/distance/time, unit conversion.
  • Reading data from tables, graphs and charts.
  • Area, volume and simple geometry.

The calculator reality

  • It’s a basic four-function calculator you click — slow. Do simple sums in your head; reserve it for multi-step numbers.
  • Learn the number-key shortcuts so you type instead of click.

Strategy that moves the needle

  • Read the question before the data. Only compute the one number asked — data sets bury a lot of decoys.
  • Guard your units. £/pence, km/m, per-week vs per-year — the trap is almost always a unit switch.
  • Estimate to eliminate. Ballpark first; if only one option is near, you may not need exact arithmetic.
  • Flag the calculation-heavy ones and clear the quick wins first.

Worked micro-example

A jacket costs £48 after a 20% discount. What was the original price?
Answer: £60. £48 is 80% of the original, so original = 48 ÷ 0.8 = £60. Not £48 × 1.2 = £57.60 — that’s the reverse-percentage trap.
⚠Adding the % back on undershoots. Divide by the decimal multiplier instead.
06
SJT

Situational Judgement

69 Q
Count
26 min
Time
23 s
Per Q

Scenarios from clinical and student life, judged against a panel of medical experts. It looks like “common sense” — which is exactly why people under-prepare and land in Band 3. Learn the examiner’s value system and Band 1 is very reachable.

What it tests

  • Integrity, empathy, teamwork and coping under pressure — not medical knowledge.
  • How closely your judgement matches the professional consensus.

Question types

  • Appropriateness: rate a response from “Very appropriate” to “Very inappropriate”.
  • Importance: rate a consideration from “Very important” to “Not important at all”.
  • Most/least appropriate from a set of options.

The examiner’s value system

  • Patient safety overrides everything. If a patient is at risk, doing nothing is never appropriate.
  • Never cover up or ignore a mistake — honesty and probity always score.
  • Escalate proportionately: usually raise it with the person first, then go to a senior if needed.
  • Stay within your competence and seek help when out of your depth — that’s a strength, not a failing.
  • Be non-judgemental and protect confidentiality.

Worked micro-example

A fellow student turns up to a hospital placement smelling of alcohol. Response: “Say nothing to avoid embarrassing them.”
Rating: Very inappropriate. Patient safety and professional standards outrank a colleague’s comfort. The appropriate path is to raise it — with them, and if needed a supervisor.
⚠Avoid the two extremes: never “ignore it”, but also rarely jump straight to reporting someone without first addressing it directly.
07

The interface & the timing doctrine

More points are lost to the clock than to difficulty. The single biggest score lever for most candidates is not knowing more — it’s never leaving a question blank and never letting one question eat the time of three.

Rule one

Answer everything

No negative marking. A blank and a wrong answer score the same — so a guess can only help. In the last seconds of every subtest, fill all remaining answers with one “banker” letter.

Rule two

Flag & move

Every subtest has a Flag button and a navigator. The moment a question runs long, flag it and go. Anchoring on one hard item is the classic way to run out of time with easy marks unanswered.

Rule three

Know your tools

Calculator (QR only, number-key shortcuts), on-screen timer, flag/navigator, and a laminated noteboard + pen at your desk. Practise with these exact tools so exam day holds no surprises.

Your per-question pace, memorised

VR ≈ 30 s · DM ≈ 60 s · QR ≈ 43 s · SJT ≈ 23 s. You won’t watch the clock every question — but you should feel when you’ve overstayed. Internalise these four numbers and “flag & move” becomes automatic.

08

The preparation plan

Six to eight focused weeks beats months of drift. The pattern that works: learn the technique first, drill weaknesses second, then live almost entirely inside full timed mocks. Review is where the score is made — analysing why each answer was wrong is worth more than doing another hundred questions.

1
Weeks 1–2 · Foundations

Learn the game before playing it

Sit one diagnostic to find your weak subtest. Learn the format and the strategy for each section (this guide). Practise untimed — you’re building correct method, not speed yet. Start daily mental-maths and speed-reading warm-ups.

2
Weeks 3–5 · Targeted drilling

Attack your weaknesses, then add the clock

Drill by subtest and question type, hardest area first. Introduce timing gradually until you hit the real per-question pace. Keep an error log — every mistake gets a one-line “why” so the same trap never catches you twice.

3
Weeks 6–8 · Full mocks

Live under exam conditions

Two to three full, timed mocks a week, in one sitting, no phone, using the on-screen calculator and noteboard. Review every mock in full. Save the official UCAT practice tests for last — they’re the truest calibration of where you’ll land.

The one habit that separates top scorers

They review more than they grind. Doing 40 questions and understanding all 40 mistakes beats doing 200 and glancing at the score. Volume without review just rehearses your errors.

10

Exam day

By now the technique is built — the job is to protect it from nerves and logistics. Nothing on the day should be a first-time experience.

Before you go

  • Sleep properly the night before — cramming past midnight costs more than it adds.
  • Bring the correct photo ID; check the test-centre rules the day before.
  • Eat something steady and hydrate; arrive early to settle your nerves.

During the test

  • Work one subtest at a time. A rough section is not the exam — reset and move on.
  • Trust your pace and your “flag & move” reflex; don’t renegotiate strategy mid-exam.
  • Always spend the final seconds of each subtest filling every blank.

Keeping nerves in check

  • Slow breathing between subtests resets a racing mind faster than re-reading a question.
  • You’ve rehearsed this exact format — the day is just another mock with higher stakes.

Perspective

  • The UCAT is one part of your application, alongside grades, personal statement and interview.
  • Different schools weight it differently — a strong score opens doors, but it isn’t the whole decision.
11

The eleven commandments

Everything above, distilled. If you internalise nothing else, internalise these.

  • Never leave a blank. No negative marking means a guess is free upside.
  • Flag and move the instant a question runs long. Protect the easy marks.
  • Answer only what’s asked — in VR and QR the data is full of decoys.
  • “Can’t Tell” is a real answer. If the passage doesn’t say it, don’t infer it.
  • Must, not might. DM rewards necessity, never mere possibility.
  • Divide, don’t add back for reverse percentages.
  • Patient safety first in every SJT scenario; never do nothing.
  • Review beats volume. Understand every mistake before doing more.
  • Live in full timed mocks for the final stretch.
  • Save the official tests for your truest calibration near the end.
  • It’s one part of the application. Prepare hard, then keep it in perspective.
